1The Tread model

Four layers stack on top of each other, and you will see them everywhere in Tread.

Everything in Tread hangs off one hierarchy: Projects, then Orders, then Jobs, then Dispatch.

  1. 1 Project is the blueprint: customer, sites, materials, and rates that the daily work inherits.
  2. 2 Order is one day of work under a project, with its drivers, equipment, and target loads or hours.
  3. 3 Job is one truck’s assignment on an order. Ten trucks means ten jobs.
  4. 4 Dispatch is how a job reaches the driver, by regular, hot, or direct send.

A few details worth holding onto:

  • Projects can run long. A day, a week, or a year. They hold the customer, the load and dump sites, the materials, and the rates.
  • Reuse the project for repeat work. Same customer and site means daily orders attach underneath one project.
  • One order carries one material. If a day involves two materials, that is two orders.

Tim’s walkthrough: how Projects, Orders, and Jobs fit together.

!
Common mistake
Do not create a new project for each day. Repeat work to the same customer and site belongs under one project, with a fresh order per day. A new project per day breaks reporting and buries your history.

3People & ecosystem

Roles decide what each person can do, and shares connect you to your customers and vendors.

Tread has seven core roles:

  • Company Admin: can do everything in the company.
  • Dispatcher: runs orders, drivers, and equipment.
  • Foreman: works their jobs and approves tickets from the field.
  • Driver: works the mobile app and sees their own assignments and pay rate only.
  • Biller: owns the approval queues, receivables, and payables.
  • Reporting: gets reports and exports.
  • Viewer: read-only.
i
Good to know
Admins can scope a role to specific sites or projects. Drivers never see customer billing rates.

Your company sits at the center as the hub that owns projects, orders, and dispatch. Three links connect you to everyone else. Break any link and the workflow it feeds stops.

  1. 1 Customer link drives invoicing. It is who you bill.
  2. 2 Vendor link drives payables and truck sharing, through a Company Share. This is a one-way link that lets trucking partners see the jobs you assign them.
  3. 3 Driver link drives dispatch and pay, for your own drivers and shared vendor drivers.
!
Common mistake
Do not create your own internal driver as a vendor record. That blocks you from assigning them jobs and leaves gaps in their pay report. Internal drivers are driver records, not vendors.

1Master-data foundation

Master data is the roster of people, trucks, places, and products that every project, order, and invoice points back to. Load it first, and load it in order.

Tread reads from nine master-data sets, and the order matters. Skipping ahead breaks references.

Why order matters: equipment types define capacity and unit of measure, so equipment cannot load until its types exist. Without that, ticket math and material tracking do not work.

i
The load order
Users → drivers → customers → vendors → equipment types → equipment → materials → sites → then departments, labels & service classes.

Two rules save you rework later:

  • Load at the parent level: master data lives at the parent level of your company hierarchy, so confirm that hierarchy before you import anything.
  • Give every record a unique Ext. ID: match it to your accounting or HR system so invoices and settlements export cleanly back to the source.
✓
Pro tip
For any set over about 20 to 30 rows, ask your implementation contact for the CSV template instead of hand-entering.
i
The rule for the whole course
You cannot build a project or dispatch an order until the master data it references already exists. Everything downstream inherits from here.
  1. 1Confirm the hierarchy so master data loads at the parent level.
  2. 2Load people first: users, then drivers.
  3. 3Load billing parties: customers, then vendors.
  4. 4Load equipment types before equipment, since capacity and UoM live on the type.
  5. 5Load materials, then sites, then any departments, labels and service classes.
  6. 6Verify in Settings: spot-check three rows per set against your source export.

2Build a Project

A project is the container for a scope of work for one customer. Build it once and its details cascade to every daily order underneath it.

The product hierarchy runs Projects → Orders → Jobs → Dispatch.

A project is a multi-day template that holds the customer, sites, materials, rates, and defaults. Each dispatch day becomes one daily order that inherits those settings, so you set things up here once instead of re-entering them every morning.

Three fields are required to create a project:

  • Customer: the party the work is for. This is the one that locks.
  • Project Name: something clear, such as "Taylor Stadium."
  • Start Date and time: when the work begins.
!
The Customer locks
Once the project is created you cannot change the customer. Pick the wrong one and you have to create a new project or contact support, so select carefully before you save.

A quote is not a separate record. It is a project in a Quoted state. You build it exactly like a live project, and pricing pulls automatically from your project rates by lane, equipment type, and material.

Quotes move by status:

  • Draft: while you build it.
  • Quoted: once shared.
  • Lost: if declined.
  • Active: once accepted. Activating turns the same record into a live project with no data recreated.
  1. 1Pick the Customer (existing or new). Confirm it, because it locks.
  2. 2Name the project and set a start date (add an end date if you have one).
  3. 3Set default pickup and drop-off sites so they pre-populate on orders.
  4. 4Add materials, rates, and order defaults (covered in the next modules).
  5. 5Set the status and hand off to Dispatch, where each day rolls up as a daily order.

How a quote is the same record as a project, just held in a Quoted status until it is accepted.

3Accounting & rates

This is where the money math lives: phase codes, materials and their prices, order defaults, tax and freight, and add-ons like fuel surcharge. Set it up once so invoices come out right.

Phase codes are the job or PO codes your customer uses to organize a project.

  • Add them your way: one at a time, or bulk-paste two columns straight from Excel.
  • Link them to materials so the right code lands on each line item.

Materials are the cargo you haul: sand, stone, asphalt, dirt. Each one carries a name, an accounting code, and a default unit of measure (tons, cubic yards, or loads).

  • One material per order, and each invoice line item maps to one material, so keep the list clean.
  • Confirm every UoM with billing before you rely on it.
  • Multiple prices per material: set per customer, per site, or globally, so the same product can price differently by context.

Rates resolve by precedence, from most to least specific:

  1. Manual override
  2. Starred rate
  3. Project rate
  4. Account rate
  5. Default

A few more pieces round out the money math:

  • Order defaults (tonnage, truck count, unit type, vehicle specs) pre-fill each order.
  • Charge-to tells the driver who is billed at the scale house.
  • Tax schedules carry accounting IDs.
  • Add-ons are the extra invoice lines: dump, broker, and leasing fees, tolls, demurrage, tarping, and standby.
!
Fuel surcharge needs all three parts
FSC needs a price index, a schedule, and an application rule. Leave one out and the surcharge silently drops off every load.
  1. 1Load phase codes and link them to materials.
  2. 2Set materials and their prices, each with a unit of measure.
  3. 3Set order defaults and charge-to so daily orders pre-fill and bill the right party.
  4. 4Add tax schedules, freight rates, and add-ons, then configure FSC in full.
  5. 5Preview the invoice lines before billing to catch anything misconfigured.

4Sites & geofences

Sites are your physical locations, and geofences are the virtual boundaries that turn a truck's GPS into arrival and departure events automatically.

Every site needs three things:

  • A name you will recognize on the board.
  • An address, which auto reverse-geocodes to a pin you can nudge if it lands off.
  • Latitude/longitude coordinates for the exact point.

Tag a site type (plant, yard, or job site), because that type drives reporting and dispatch defaults. Set default pickup and drop-off sites on the project so they pre-populate on every order, and even a default site with no fixed drop-off still gets geofence tracking.

Every site carries a geofence. You choose the shape:

  • Circle (the default): just needs a radius. Works well for pits and plants.
  • Polygon: tighter accuracy at large or oddly shaped sites, and supports KML/GeoJSON imports.

A geofence timer sets the minimum dwell time before a pass counts as a real arrival, which filters out trucks that only drive by.

i
How tracking works
When a truck crosses in, Tread logs an arrival. When it crosses out, the load closes and the cycle-time clock starts for the next one.

5Conditional setup

Some setup only applies to certain jobs. Turn on prevailing wage and set note visibility tiers when the work calls for it, and leave them off when it does not.

Prevailing wage is a government-mandated pay system (base pay, fringe, and overtime rules) for public-works projects. It currently covers states like California, Nevada, Minnesota, and New York, plus federal Davis-Bacon work.

Enabling it takes two separate steps that do not happen automatically:

  1. 1Flag the project as prevailing wage.
  2. 2Flag each material as PW, separately. The project flag alone does not cover materials.

A few things to know once it is on:

  • Classification follows the truck: when a driver switches trucks mid-shift, the rate changes with the equipment, not the person.
  • Tread suggests a classification based on truck type and work, and the dispatcher or biller confirms or overrides it.
  • A prevailing-wage badge shows on the order.
  • The requester field records who asked for the work.
!
Common mistake
Missing the material PW flag under-bills the job. Flagging the project alone does not cover the materials, so verify both flags are set.

Notes and attachments carry visibility tiers so the right people see the right thing: project, order, driver, and internal.

  • Internal notes stay with your team.
  • A rate attachment or driver-tier note is shared out to drivers.

Set the tier deliberately so you never expose internal or pricing detail to a driver by accident.

6Segmentation & units

Departments, service classes, and units of measure are how you slice, filter, and report on your work. All three are optional, so turn on only what your team actually uses.

Departments are a company-level grouping you put on orders, projects, and equipment to segment your operation by division, yard, or region.

  • Best when your finance team already reports by division.
  • Set the list in Settings, then filter the Dispatch board and Reports by department to focus on one slice.
  • Keep departments broad.

6bService Classes

Service classes segment work by type: aggregate hauling, demolition, long-haul, ready-mix.

  • Tag each order with its class to filter the board.
  • Feeds job-cost coding into your accounting exports.
  • Department vs. service class: department answers who or where, service class answers what kind of work.

6cUnits of Measure

Tread supports many units, but you only need a handful.

  • Enable what you use in Settings (commonly Yards, Tons, Pounds, Loads, and Hours) and disable the rest so dispatch and rate dropdowns stay clean.
  • Cubic yards appear as "Yard."
  • Keep UoM consistent across materials and equipment so ticket math works.
i
Department vs. Service Class
Department = who or where (division, yard, region). Service Class = what kind of work. Avoid overlapping tags for the same idea, and test by creating a sample order to confirm the new options show up on the form.
!
Common mistake
Using free-form labels for something that belongs in a structured field. If you are categorizing by department, service class, or equipment type, use that dedicated field so it flows into reporting and accounting correctly.

2Build & send an order

Building an order is where you tell Tread what to haul, how much, and how the trucks should run.

An order is one delivery assignment: one material, from one pickup to one dropoff.

  • The hierarchy: Projects, then Orders, then Jobs, then Dispatch.
  • One material each: a project can hold many orders, but every order carries a single material.
  • Set it on the order card: material, pickup and dropoff, quantity, units, and how the trucks run.

How the order runs is set by three dispatch modes, driven by the Units and Running fields:

  1. 1Loads. Set Units to Load, enter a Quantity, and leave Running unchecked. Each driver hauls exactly that many loads, and the order ends when the last load drops.
  2. 2Running. Set Units to Load, leave Quantity empty, and check Running. Trucks cycle load after load with no fixed end. You can also enter a Quantity with Running checked to give each truck a target it hits before continuing.
  3. 3Tonnage. Set Units to Ton and enter the tons per truck. The order is measured by weight rather than by a load count.

Truck type and capacity feed the math behind the order:

  • Capacity: set in Settings. Tread uses it to auto-calculate quantities and cycles when there is no scale ticket.
  • Rates: a rate card auto-fills the pricing for the order.
  • Delivery estimate: calculated from the equipment capacity you selected.

3Assign & manage

Once an order is built, you assign trucks to it, send those jobs to phones, and manage the changes that come up during the day.

You assign a driver or vendor to each job row, then send it. The single most important choice is which tab you assign from:

  • Driver tab: your own drivers, and Owner-Operators you dispatch directly.
  • Vendor tab: hauling companies that sub-dispatch to their own drivers.

That choice maps to three routing modes:

  • Regular: sends the load to your own drivers and company trucks. It appears on their phone immediately.
  • Hot: sends the load to an outside vendor's dispatcher, who picks which of their drivers accepts it. The driver name shows on your board only after acceptance.
  • Direct: assign straight to an Owner-Operator's driver, once they have shared that driver with you.
  1. 1Open the order and find the job row you want to fill.
  2. 2Click Assign and choose the Driver tab or the Vendor tab.
  3. 3Search and select by name or truck number.

To move faster on repeat work:

  • Duplicate: carries an order's driver assignments forward.
  • Copy-and-edit: tweak the details before you create the order.
  • Foreman: assign one to an order from mobile so they can see it in the field. A foreman sees the order but cannot dispatch trucks.

4AI & tickets

Tickets are the proof each load happened. Tread makes them digital and uses AI to chase down the ones that are missing.

A ticket is a record of a load. Digital tickets replace paper, so nothing has to be scanned, faxed, or driven back to the office.

Each ticket captures:

  • The load: load number, material, quantity, and unit of measure.
  • The times: load and dump times.
  • The proof: a photo and its source.
i
Good to know
You can let vendors split jobs so their portion of the work carries its own tickets.

Tickets reach Tread three ways:

  • In the app: a driver or foreman photographs a paper ticket.
  • By text: a driver texts a photo to a Tread SMS number. Handy for drivers without the app.
  • By integration: a scale house, quarry, or plant sends the ticket automatically, with no driver action at all.

The Ticket Chaser is Tread's AI agent. It reads each ticket image and matches it to the dispatched load:

  • Extracts: the ticket number, weight, material, and times.
  • Flags: anything ambiguous gets sent to a person to review.
  • Chases: texts drivers to collect and verify missing tickets. They reply right from their phone by text or photo, which removes the manual chase.
i
Where to turn it on
Enable the Ticket Chaser under Settings, then Agent Configurations.

5Dispatch power features

These are the levers that separate a smooth dispatch from a chaotic one. Learn them once and you will reach for them every day.

Running vs. fixed. How an order ends depends on its type.

  • Running: drivers keep cycling load after load until you stop them. It never auto-ends. Use it when you do not know the total up front (haul until the pile is gone).
  • Fixed load-count: auto-ends after the last load is dropped. Use it when the quantity is set.
!
Common mistake
Running orders never auto-end. Stop the order when the work is done, or drivers will keep cycling.

Schedule by load, charge by ton. Your dispatch target and your billing unit do not have to match.

  • Track by load: simple for drivers.

1Field specialties

These are the field workflows that only some operations run.

Turn one on when the way your trucks move in the real world stops matching a simple point-to-point haul.

i
When to turn these on
You need none of these to dispatch a standard order. Reach for one only when your operation has the exact pattern it solves, and leave the rest off so your setup stays simple.
  1. 1 Paving and moving geofences. For linear work (paving, milling, striping), the geofence follows the paver down the road, so trucks are tracked at the active drop zone instead of a fixed point. Mark the order as paving work, set the paver as the source equipment, and keep the radius tight (roughly 50 to 150 feet).
  2. 2 Back-hauls and return runs. When a driver delivers outbound and picks up different material on the way back, Tread tracks the round trip as two separate billable tickets on one dispatch. Set the outbound material as primary, add the return material, and price each leg in the Rates, Add-Ons & Fees section.
  3. 3 Mixed loads. When one truck carries material for several customers on one trip, split the load so each delivery gets its own ticket and its own bill. Enable split-load support in platform settings, turn on Split Load per order, and give each customer their own quantity.
  4. 4 Plant-to-plant hauls. When both the pickup plant and the delivery plant are already on your Tread account, scale tickets and receiving docs route to the load automatically. Connect both plants as customers or vendors, set the upstream plant as pickup and the downstream as delivery, and confirm both operating windows line up.
!
Common mistake
Two setup gaps quietly break these workflows:
  • An oversized paving radius makes trucks queued behind the paver read as on-site, inflating wait time and load counts. Start the radius conservatively.
  • Running a mixed load without an Equipment Type capacity means Tread cannot validate the split totals. Set capacity first.

3Compliance & docs

Compliance documents prove a driver or vendor is legal to be on a job, and Tread enforces it before dispatch so you are not tracking expirations by hand.

A compliance document is a file attached to a driver or vendor with an expiration date. Tread watches those dates and can block or warn on dispatch when a required doc is missing or expired.

  • What counts: DOT card, insurance certificate, CDL, drug screen, W-9.
  • Where it matters most: public-works, union, and large general-contractor jobs where you have to show the paperwork.
  • How it runs: set it up once in master data, then let the driver and vendor portals do the uploading.
  1. 1 Define document types in Compliance settings (for example Insurance, CDL, Drug Screen) with an expiration policy for each.
  2. 2 Set who needs what by marking which documents are required for drivers and which for vendors.
  3. 3 Choose the enforcement per type: block dispatch outright, or warn and allow.
  4. 4 Let drivers and vendors upload through their own portals, and review expirations on a set schedule.
!
Common mistake
Three things trip people up:
  • Vendor documents are owned by the vendor, not by you, so you cannot upload a missing one on their behalf. Set that expectation during onboarding.
  • An expiration blocks the next dispatch, not the current shift, so a doc that lapses mid-shift will not pull an active driver.
  • Run an expiration review weekly to avoid Monday-morning surprises.
